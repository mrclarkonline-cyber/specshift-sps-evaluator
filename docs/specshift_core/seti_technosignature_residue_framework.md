# SETI / Technosignature Residue Framework

Date created: 2026-06-27
Status: Draft v0.1
Audience: Internal / public-research-safe
Scope: Uncertainty-first framework for weak, passive, ambiguous, and multi-channel technosignature residue review.

## One-Sentence Summary

This lane explores whether public or research-grade observation traces can be reviewed for weak, passive, or ambiguous technosignature residue without claiming detection, origin, intent, or extraterrestrial intelligence.

## Core Idea

Traditional SETI often emphasizes messages.

This framework asks whether some technosignatures may look less like messages and more like residue.

Safe concept line:

> We looked for messages; we should also ask whether some signals would look more like metabolism.

Here, “metabolism” means persistent, structured, energy-using, environment-interacting activity.

It does not mean proof of life.

It does not mean proof of intelligence.

It does not mean proof of extraterrestrial technology.

## Corrected Status

This lane is exploratory.

It has not detected extraterrestrial intelligence.

It has not validated a technosignature.

It has not established nonhuman origin for any signal.

Any future claim must remain uncertainty-first and source-grounded.

A review output may identify:

- Candidate anomaly.

## Central Boundary

An anomaly is not a technosignature.

A technosignature candidate is not proof of technology.

A signal pattern is not proof of intelligence.

A residue hypothesis is not proof of origin.

## Candidate Signal Types

Possible categories:

### Weak signal

A low-strength signal that may be lost in noise or ordinary background variation.

### Passive residue

A possible trace of activity not structured as a deliberate message.

### Ambiguous repetition

A recurring pattern that may have natural, instrumental, or data-processing explanations.

### Cross-channel candidate

An observation that appears in more than one channel or source, but still does not establish origin.

### Environmental interaction candidate

A signal that may suggest interaction with an environment, but requires strong alternative-explanation review.

### Data-quality artifact

A candidate likely explained by sensor, pipeline, timestamp, selection, or processing issues.

## Classification Ladder

Use conservative categories:

### Level 0 — Ordinary / explained

Consistent with known ordinary explanation.

### Level 1 — Data-quality concern

Possibly caused by artifact, noise, pipeline issue, duplicated record, or bad metadata.

### Level 2 — Ambiguous anomaly

Unusual but insufficient to prioritize.

### Level 3 — Candidate residue pattern

Potentially interesting structure, but origin unknown.

### Level 4 — Cross-source candidate

Appears across multiple sources or modalities, still origin-uncertain.

### Level 5 — Follow-up priority

Warrants expert review, source validation, and targeted follow-up.

No level should be treated as proof of extraterrestrial intelligence.

## False-Positive Doctrine

False positives are expected.

Possible ordinary explanations include:

- Instrument artifact.
- Processing pipeline artifact.
- Human-made interference.
- Satellite or aircraft contamination.
- Atmospheric effects.
- Natural astrophysical source.
- Selection bias.
- Confirmation bias.
- Duplicate data.
- Calibration error.
- Timestamp error.
- Coordinate error.
- Publication or reporting artifact.

## Required Report Structure

A safe report should include:

1. Observation summary.
2. Source list.
3. Data quality notes.
4. Classification level.
5. Alternative explanations.
6. Cross-source status.
7. What would strengthen the case.
8. What would weaken the case.
9. Current interpretation.
10. Next review step.

Required conclusion style:

> This is a candidate observation for review. It does not establish extraterrestrial intelligence, technology, origin, intent, or nonhuman involvement.

## “Metabolism, Not Message” Explanation

A message-seeking approach asks:

> Is someone deliberately transmitting information to us?

A residue-seeking approach asks:

> Would sustained activity leave observable traces even if no one intended to communicate?

That shift may expand the search space.

But it also increases false-positive risk.

Therefore the framework must be conservative, source-grounded, and uncertainty-first.
