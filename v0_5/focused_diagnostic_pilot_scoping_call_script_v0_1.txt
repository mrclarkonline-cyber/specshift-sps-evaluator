Focused Diagnostic Pilot Scoping Call Script v0.1
Internal call script. Not legal-reviewed. Not buyer-ready final copy.

Purpose:
Convert a positive outreach reply into a concrete focused diagnostic pilot conversation.

Standing boundary line:
SpecShift Architectural Logic Diagnostics is a pre-deployment diagnostic and architecture-stage review process. It is not a benchmark, certification, compliance audit, runtime monitor, security tool, deployment approval, or guarantee of AI safety.

Call length:
20 minutes.

Call goal:
Identify one concrete AI-agent workflow where a bounded first-pass diagnostic review could create practical value.

Opening script:
Thanks for taking the time. The goal today is simple: identify whether there is one concrete AI-agent workflow where a focused diagnostic review would be useful. I am not here to certify anything or ask for production access. I am trying to understand the workflow, the inputs, the handoffs, and where silent failure would be expensive.

Agenda:
1. Confirm the boundary: pre-deployment diagnostic only.
2. Identify one candidate workflow.
3. Map the workflow's inputs, systems, handoffs, and approval points.
4. Identify where weak evidence, stale state, unclear scope, or false completion would matter.
5. Decide whether a bounded paid diagnostic pilot is worth scoping.

Scoping questions:
1. What AI-agent workflow are you considering, prototyping, or already testing?
2. What systems or data sources does the workflow touch?
3. What inputs does the agent receive, and which of those inputs are treated as trusted?
4. What actions can the agent recommend, initiate, or complete?
5. Where are human approvals, handoffs, or review points currently required?
6. What happens if data is stale, incomplete, conflicting, or low-fidelity?
7. How is task completion verified?
8. What rollback, recovery, or escalation path exists if the workflow fails or becomes ambiguous?

Fit signals:
- There is one concrete workflow, not just a broad AI strategy discussion.
- The workflow has meaningful operational, customer, financial, or trust consequences if it fails silently.
- The buyer can provide enough non-production context to support synthetic review.
- The workflow is early enough that design changes are still possible.
- The buyer understands the review is diagnostic, not certification.

No-fit signals:
- The buyer wants certification, compliance sign-off, runtime monitoring, cybersecurity testing, or deployment approval.
- There is no concrete workflow to review.
- The buyer wants free exploratory consulting.
- The buyer cannot share even bounded workflow context.
- The buyer expects a guarantee of safety, reliability, or production outcome.

Deliverables preview:
- Executive findings memo
- Failure-pattern review against selected synthetic scenarios
- Limitation and boundary map
- Handoff, evidence, escalation, and recovery-path gap list
- Recommended design-review next steps

Business-continuity answer:
For a pilot, the scope, questions, deliverables, and review outputs are documented and version-controlled. The engagement would be milestone-based, with a defined off-ramp if continuity or scope issues prevent delivery. I would not ask a buyer to rely on an undocumented black box or an open-ended founder-only process.

Pricing language:
For a first conversation, do not lead with a number. Say: pricing depends on scope, access, and deliverables. The first step is deciding whether there is one workflow worth scoping as a bounded paid diagnostic pilot.

Close script:
Based on this conversation, the next step would be to select one workflow and define a narrow pilot scope: what materials are available, which failure patterns matter most, what outputs would be useful, and what decision the review needs to support. If that sounds useful, I can send a short pilot scope outline after this call.

Follow-up:
Send a short follow-up summarizing the selected workflow, likely failure patterns, proposed deliverables, boundary statement, and next step for a pilot scope outline.

Failure column:
This script stops fitting if the conversation shifts into certification, compliance approval, runtime protection, legal advice, HR decision-making, cybersecurity testing, deployment approval, or guaranteed safety. In those cases, restate the boundary or decline the fit.
